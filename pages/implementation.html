Plantation Cycle

Black pepper (Piper nigrum), often known as the “King of Spices” or “Black Gold,” is a significant spice crop gaining prominence in the northeastern region of India. Typically, cultivation takes place in home gardens and homesteads where the plant is allowed to trail on shade-providing trees such as coconut and areca nut palms.[1] Recommended black pepper cultivars for this region include Karimunda, Panniyur-1, Panniyur-2, Panchami, and Poumami.[2]

Growth and Branching Pattern of Black Pepper Vine

  1. Main Stem Originate from a seed or from a stem cutting. It climbs on a support with the aid of aerial or adventitious roots.
  2. Runner Shoots Produced from the basal portion of the main stem, growing at right angle to the main stem, usually restricted up to 50 cm from the ground
  3. Fruiting branches (Plagiotropes) Produced from the nodes of the main stem, they grow laterally more or less at right angles to the main stem, bearing the spikes.
  4. Topshoots (Orthotropes) After a period of vertical growth, the top portion of the main shoots attains a bushy appearance with shorter, thicker internodes and profuse branching with a large number of adventitious roots at the nodes.
  5. Hanging shoots (Geotropes) In a fully grown vine, some of the plagiotropes at the top portion are seen to give rise to a special type of shoots which hang down and grow geotropically. [2]

With an optimal growing range between 25°C and 35°C, the plant thrives in warm and humid tropical climates, tolerating a wide temperature range of 10°C to 40°C. Black pepper grows best in friable loam soils rich in humus and organic matter. These soils should be well-drained and have a pH range of 4.5 to 6.0 for ideal growth.[1]

Propagation involves planting stem cuttings, usually near live standards such as Erythrina indica or Grevillea robusta, at the onset of pre-monsoon showers during April or May. A spacing of 2.7 meters by 2.7 meters is the optimum planting density, which allows for about 1,300 plants per hectare. To aid in the plants' early establishment, especially during dry spells, adequate irrigation and mulching are crucial.[1]

Nutrient management includes the application of organic manures such as compost or cattle dung at a rate of 10 kg per plant annually. Additionally, recommended fertilizers such as urea (100 g), rock phosphate (300 g), and muriate of potash (100 g) per plant are applied in two equal split doses, one during April-May and the other during August-September. Proper plant training and pruning to manage vine growth and maintain plant health are also vital.[1]

Harvesting begins in the third or fourth year after planting. The vines typically flower in May-June, with the berries maturing over 6-8 months. Harvesting is conducted between late November and early February when the berries turn red or yellowish-red. The yield per well-managed vine averages 2 to 2.5 kg of black pepper.[1]

Pests and diseases are common challenges in black pepper cultivation. Major pests include the top shoot borer (Cydia hemidoxa) and leaf gall thrips (Liothrips karnyi). Common diseases include foot rot caused by Phytophthora capsici and anthracnose caused by Colletotrichum gloeosporioides. Effective pest and disease management involves applying recommended insecticides, and fungicides, and adopting cultural practices such as ensuring proper drainage to prevent water stagnation.[1]
