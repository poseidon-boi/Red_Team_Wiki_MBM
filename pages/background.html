Introduction

Phytophthora capsici is a highly destructive oomycete pathogen that causes foot rot disease in black pepper (Piper nigrum), leading to severe economic losses in pepper-producing regions. Traditional management strategies have included chemical fungicides, cultural practices, and resistant cultivars that often fall short due to pathogen adaptability, environmental concerns, and resistance development [1].

Phytophthora species are plant pathogens that use a combination of motility, attachment, and effector proteins to infect and suppress host immune responses. These pathogens produce biflagellated zoospores, which swim through water films to locate host plants before encysting and germinating. The infection process begins with the formation of appressoria, specialized structures that help penetrate plant tissues. Once inside, Phytophthora forms intercellular hyphae and haustoria, allowing it to extract nutrients and deliver effectors into the plant [1].

To address this issue, we looked into the following before finalising our final solution to the project:

NLP Effectors

NLPs are a class of cytolytic effectors secreted by filamentous pathogens, including P. capsici. These proteins, such as NLP10, have been shown to induce necrosis and ethylene production, disrupting plant cell integrity. NLPs selectively target dicotyledonous plants, damaging their plasma membranes and causing cell death, which facilitates pathogen invasion. In P. capsici, NLP10 is thought to be involved in the early stages of infection, weakening host tissues before other effectors take over. In NCBI , the enymes are mostly searched upon the name “PcNLPX” where X denotes the number [7].

Elicitins

Plays a dual role in Phytophthora pathogenicity and also acting cell death inducing proteins acting both as sterol-binding proteins essential for pathogen survival and as immune-modulating effectors. Since Phytophthora species cannot synthesize sterols, elicitins such as Capsicein from P. capsici bind host-derived sterols, supporting pathogen growth. Additionally, they may function as pathogen-associated molecular patterns (PAMPs) that trigger plant immunity by activating systemic acquired resistance (SAR), hypersensitive response (HR), reactive oxygen species (ROS) production, and defense gene expression. Capsicein, a 10 kDa elicitin from Phytophthora capsici, triggers immune responses in non-host plants by interacting with NgRLK1, a receptor-like kinase in Nicotiana glutinosa [7].

However, certain elicitins also act as virulence factors that suppress plant immunity, facilitating host colonization. Knockdown studies, such as the suppression of α-plurivorin in P. plurivora, have demonstrated that reducing elicitin expression weakens Phytophthora virulence, preventing infection in some hosts. Conversely, overexpression can either enhance plant resistance, as seen with PcINF1, or increase pathogen virulence, depending on the plant-pathogen interaction. Research indicates that PcINF1 interacts with the pepper protein SRC2-1, a receptor-like cytoplasmic kinase. This interaction is crucial for initiating immune responses in pepper plants. Additionally, the protein SGT1 has been found to be involved in the PcINF1/SRC2-1-induced immunity, suggesting a complex signaling pathway leading to defense responses [3][4].

Despite their crucial roles, elicitins are not always essential for infection, as their primary function in some species is sterol acquisition rather than immune suppression.

RxLR Effectors

Another major class of effectors used by P. capsici is the RxLR (Arginine-any amino acid-Leucine-Arginine) family. These effectors function by entering plant cells and directly interfering with host immunity. F RXLR effectors from P. capsici has been found to target and inhibit central immune kinases, preventing the activation of immune responses. This allows P. capsici to evade detection and continue its infection cycle without being attacked by the plant's defense system [10].

Protein Phosphatase 2A (PP2A) as a Decoy Receptor

Phytophthora capsici deploys RXLR-class effectors that enter plant cells and manipulate host immunity and plant resistance. One such effector, PcAvh1, has been found to target Protein Phosphatase 2A (PP2A), a crucial regulator of plant defense signaling. By binding to PP2A, PcAvh1 suppresses plant immune responses, making the host more susceptible to infection. We ran the NCBI databases to check if the PP2A is expressed by any bacteria. We couldn't find any and thus resorted to finding an alternative to the PP2A called PrPE that was said to be structurally similar and functional to PP2A through literature review. The PrPE was expressed or produced by the ori yjbP gene from Bacillus subtilis 168. The sequence of PP2A and PrPE was aligned in Pymol to find if they are structurally similar. We docked the RXLR receptors and PrPE to check the binding affinity. Unfortunately the docking results were not favourable and thus had to eliminate the idea [9].

a. β-1,3-Glucanase:

β-1,3-glucanase is an enzyme that hydrolyzes β-1,3-glucans, a major structural component of the Phytophthora capsici cell wall. Since P. capsici contains more β-1,3-glucans compared to true fungi (which have higher chitin content), this enzyme weakens its cell wall, reducing virulence or causing cell lysis [11].

b. Sphingolipid-Targeting Compounds:

Sphingolipids are vital components of P. capsici cell membranes, essential for maintaining cellular integrity and signaling. Targeting sphingolipids can disrupt these critical functions, weakening the pathogen. For instance, a compound called 2E,4E-decadienoic acid, produced by a coculture of Bacillus subtilis and Trichoderma asperellum, exhibited a 94.21% inhibition rate against pathogens. Although the exact producer remains unidentified, this strategy highlights the potential for novel antifungal agents [12].

c. Iturin A :

Iturin A is a lipopeptide antibiotic produced by Bacillus subtilis that exhibits potent antifungal activity. It disrupts fungal cell membranes by forming pores, leading to ion leakage and cell death. Its mechanism of action targets the lipid bilayer, making it highly effective against a wide range of fungal pathogens, including Phytophthora capsici. By compromising the integrity of the pathogen's cell membrane, Iturin A weakens P. capsici's virulence and contributes to its suppression.

These compounds are naturally produced by B. subtilis are engineered for inducible expression, activated only by when PcAvh1 is detected. The response regulator (activated via TCS) binds to an engineered promoter that controls antifungal biosynthesis genes. Upon PcAvh1 binding, antifungal production is induced, along expression.

Since the Plant Cells also contain glucan and Iturin A is broad spectrum, it could induce plant cell death on usage of β-1,3-Glucanase. Iturin A is broad spectrum antifungal , since the main mode of infection occurs in the roots, injecting an antifungal could cause the beneficial fungi to also perish thus collapsing the fungal or microbiotic ecosystem.
