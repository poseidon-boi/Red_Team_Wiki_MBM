Impact and vision

Foot rot, caused by Phytophthora capsici , is a significant disease affecting black pepper (Piper nigrum L. ) cultivation, leading to substantial yield and economic losses globally. The impact of this disease varies across different regions and is influenced by factors such as environmental conditions and management practices. In India, particularly in the states of Karnataka and Kerala, foot rot is recognized as a major constraint in black pepper production. A survey conducted in the Shivamogga and Chickmagaluru districts of Karnataka reported disease incidences ranging from 50% to 65% in certain villages, leading to significant yield reductions [1].

Another survey in Karnataka revealed varying disease incidences across districts, with the highest mean incidence of 18.44% recorded in Hassan district. Foot rot incidence was notably higher during the 2017 growing season (16.38%) compared to the previous year (14.35%). The disease typically causes a 5-20% loss of vines, but in some individual gardens, losses can reach up to 95%. [2]. In Kerala, studies have estimated annual yield losses due to foot rot ranging from 3.4% to 9.4%. For instance, between 1982 and 1985, Calicut and Kannur districts reported losses of 119 and 905 tonnes of black pepper, respectively, attributed to this disease [3]. On a global scale, Phytophthora capsici is a major threat to black pepper cultivation, with varying incidences reported in different countries. In Vietnam, foot rot is considered the most devastating disease of black pepper, leading to annual crop losses ranging from 5% to 95%. The disease incidence is highest during the period from May to November [4]. In Indonesia, foot rot has caused a degradation of pepper production from 5% to 0%, with yield declines reaching up to 30% to 40% in some gardens, and in severe cases, it can devastate 100% of the crop [5]. Similarly, in Malaysia, more than 95% of pepper farms are infected with P. capsici, leading to a 5% to 10% decrease in productivity [6].

The disease is not limited to Southeast Asia but is also a concern in other parts of the world, including the Americas, Europe, Africa, and Australia. Foot rot affects solanaceous and cucurbitaceous plants, often leading to complete crop loss. Researchers have noted that foot rot disease can lead to rapid and devastating crop failure, especially under conducive environmental conditions [7]. On a global scale, foot rot has been associated with annual crop losses estimated between $4.5 million and $7.5 million. In countries like Vietnam, losses due to quick wilt have been estimated to range from 15-20%, resulting in an average yield loss of 30-50% [8].

Life cycle of P.capsici

P. capsici reproduces by both sexual and asexual means. The pathogen produces two mating types called A1 and A2. Each mating type produces hormones responsible for gametangia differentiation in the opposite mating type.P. capsici produces a female gametangium called oogonium and a male gametangium called the antheridium. Meiosis occurs within the gametangia resulting in the of oospores. These are the sexual spores that can germinate either directly through the formation of a germ tube or indirectly through sporangium. This pathogen also reproduces asexually by sporangia. Sporangia are generally ovoid and consist of a papillum at their apex. Sporangia germinate to produce zoospores which help in the infection of host cells [9].

Infection Cycle : If the conditions are favourable the P. capsici produces many sporangia. During irrigation events, mature sporangia release motile zoospores. These zoospores are negatively geotropic (against gravity or upwards) and swim chemotactically towards plants. Once these zoospores reach the surface of the plant, they penetrate the host cell by the appressorium to form intercellular hyphae. This intracellular hyphae is also called an infection vesicle. This infection vesicle further matures to form haustorium. It then secretes effectors like RxLRs and CRNs. These effectors inhibit key plant protease namely Phytophthora Inhibited Protease (PIP1) Hevea brasiliensis serine protease (HbSPA) soybean aspartic protease(GMAP1) soybean glucanase (GMGIP1) [10].

Current strategies

1. Chemical Fungicides : Chemical fungicides are commonly used for managing Phytophthora capsici infection in black pepper. Treatments with a variety of commercial fungicides, such as Sectin (Fenamidone + Mancozeb), Acrobat Mz (Dimethomorph + Mancozeb), Curzate M8 (Cymoxanil + Mancozeb), and Kocide (Copper hydroxide), have shown significant efficacy in reducing disease severity. Studies have demonstrated that the application of Fenamidone + Mancozeb and Copper hydroxide resulted in up to 78% reduction in disease intensity compared to untreated control plants, with the best results observed in the 2013-2014 season [11]. Despite their effectiveness, chemical fungicides have limitations, such as the development of resistance and environmental concerns like copper accumulation in soil and toxicity to beneficial microorganisms [12]. Repeated application of these fungicides is often necessary to maintain effectiveness, and resistance to certain fungicides, such as metalaxyl, has already been documented in India and globally [13].

2. Biological Control Agents : In recent years, biological control agents have been explored as an alternative or supplement to chemical fungicides. Organisms such as Trichoderma harzianum and Bacillus subtilis have shown promise in controlling Phytophthora capsici in laboratory settings, often reducing disease by 50-70%. However, field efficacy has been inconsistent due to environmental variability, such as moisture levels and microbial competition [14]. One biopesticide approach involving a consortium of plant growth-promoting microorganisms (PGPM) has shown moderate success, with disease reduction rates of around 24.3% in some trials, which is significantly better than untreated controls [15]. Despite these promising results, biological control agents require precise timing and application methods to be effective and have limited activity against specific life stages of Phytophthora capsici.

3. Resistant Pepper Varieties : Another strategy involves using resistant pepper cultivars, such as Pusa Jwala and IISR Avinash, which offer partial resistance to Phytophthora capsici. These varieties can delay infection but do not provide complete immunity. Resistance can break down under high pathogen pressure or with new Phytophthora strains. Furthermore, farmers often prefer high-yielding varieties that may not possess adequate resistance [16]. Breeding programs for developing resistant varieties are slow, often taking 5-10 years per cultivar, and may not offer a quick solution to the problem.

4. Cultural Practices : Cultural practices such as raised beds, crop rotation, and soil solarization are used to reduce disease incidence. Raised beds help with drainage and prevent root rot, while crop rotation can help break the pathogen's lifecycle. However, these practices are not curative and are labor-intensive, making them less feasible for smallholder farmers with limited resources [17]. While they can reduce disease incidence, they do not completely eliminate soil-borne inoculum, and their effectiveness can be affected by weather conditions, such as heavy rains that may negate drainage efforts [18].
