SCIENCE AND PEPPER

Black Pepper Foot Rot: a silent killer of india's spice gold

Black pepper, often called the “king of spices,” plays a crucial role in global trade and cuisine. However, farmers face a persistent challenge: foot rot, a devastating disease caused by Phytophthora capsici.

Foot Rot is caused by Phytophthora Capsici, a soilborne pathogen that thrives in warm, humid conditions. The infection typically begins with wilting leaves and water soaked lesions on the stem, progressing to collar rot and root decay. As the disease advances, the plant's vascular system becomes compromised, preventing the uptake of water and nutrients. without intervention, the vine eventually succumbs, leaving behind barren fields.

When an infection takes hold, entire vines can be lost, leading to significant economic setbacks.

In karnataka, disease incidence reaches up to 65% in some regions, with yield losses exceeding 50%. historical data from kerala indicates that annual losses have surpassed 0 tonnes in affected districts, disrupting both local economies and international supply chains.

Over time, phytophthora capsici has shown the ability to develop resistance to commonly used fungicides. this adaptability often referred to as the “fighter for ecstasy” phenomenon by cultivator, implies that traditional treatments become less effective with repeated use.

Black pepper isn't just a spice—it's a billion-dollar industry with deep cultural and economic significance. India, Vietnam, and Indonesia dominate global production, but foot rot is spreading. As climate change intensifies rainfall and humidity, the perfect breeding ground for Phytophthora expands. The result? unstable pepper prices, declining yields, and a crisis that's only just beginning.

We aim to engineer a bacterium with high specificity against Phytophthora capsici using an effector-receptor mechanism to trigger localized fengycin overexpression, ensuring a precise, mediated antifungal response while preserving beneficial microbes.
